MaxMI: A Maximal Mutual Information Criterion for Manipulation Concept Discovery

Pei Zhou1, Yanchao Yang1,
1The University of Hong Kong
Paper arXiv Code

TL;DR: We propose an information-theoretic approach for discovering key physical states in unannotated robotic demonstrations. By using a Maximal Mutual Information (MaxMI) criterion, we bypass the need for human semantics and labeling, allowing a network to accurately identify key states that enhance manipulation policy training and improve generalization.

Abstract

We aim to discover manipulation concepts embedded in the unannotated demonstrations, which are recognized as key physical states. The discovered concepts can facilitate training manipulation policies and promote generalization. Current methods relying on multimodal foundation models for deriving key states usually lack accuracy and semantic consistency due to limited multimodal robot data.

In contrast, we introduce an information-theoretic criterion to characterize the regularities that signify a set of physical states. We also develop a framework that trains a concept discovery network using this criterion, thus bypassing the dependence on human semantics and alleviating costly human labeling.

The proposed criterion is based on the observation that key states, which deserve to be conceptualized, often admit more physical constraints than non-key states. This phenomenon can be formalized as maximizing the mutual information between the putative key state and its preceding state, i.e., Maximal Mutual Information (MaxMI).

By employing MaxMI, the trained key state localization network can accurately identify states of sufficient physical significance, exhibiting reasonable semantic compatibility with human perception. Furthermore, the proposed framework produces key states that lead to concept-guided manipulation policies with higher success rates and better generalization in various robotic tasks compared to the baselines, verifying the effectiveness of the proposed criterion.

Highlights

Mutual information between a random state variable and its preceding one achieves a maximum when the state variable coincides with a key state (manipulation concept), as verified across four manipulation tasks.

The subfigures highlight the moments when the mutual information arrives at a peak, together with images that illustrate the corresponding key states.

Mutual information criterion

We choose a neural estimator, InfoNet, due to its efficiency, robustness, and differentiability, which is critical for training the proposed self-supervised concept discovery pipeline. And the neural estimator is pretrained and fixed.

Key state localization

The proposed Key State Localization Network (KSL-Net) for manipulation concept discovery. Every key concept (to be discovered and localized) is represented by a learnable embedding; the concept embedding is then appended to all state vectors along a trajectory. These augmented state vectors are further processed by a neural network to derive the probability of each state being the identified key state.

Experiments

The discovered key concepts are consistent with human preference.

Moreover, the discovered concepts prove to be more beneficial than others when applied on top of the CoTPC policy.

The number of key concepts will affect the performance of the policy. It's not the case that more key concepts will lead a better performance.
